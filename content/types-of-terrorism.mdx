Domestic and foreign terrorism differ in where planning occurs, how networks are connected, and what broader context shapes the threat. Both forms can cause serious harm.

<Info>
  Both domestic and foreign terrorism use fear and violence to influence others, so both require awareness, prevention, and responsible reporting.
</Info>

## Two main types

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Domestic Terrorism" icon="house">
    Domestic terrorism involves violent acts planned or carried out mainly within one country. These acts often target the government, the public, or specific groups inside that nation.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Foreign Terrorism" icon="globe">
    Foreign terrorism refers to violent acts linked to groups or networks that operate across national borders. These actions may be directed by outside organizations or shaped by international conflicts.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

## Quick comparison

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Planning Context" icon="map">
    Domestic cases are usually tied to issues inside one country, while foreign cases often involve outside groups or international conflict.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Network Pattern" icon="network-wired">
    Domestic networks may stay local or national. Foreign networks may connect people, money, or propaganda across borders.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Shared Risk" icon="triangle-exclamation">
    Both forms use fear and violence to influence others, so both require awareness, prevention, and responsible reporting.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
